Because perspective students, parents, faculty, and University administrators use rankings to make decisions, improving their accuracy is important. This tool seeks to inform the ranking process with better quantitive metrics on publications. This tool is incomplete because it needs to be combined with qualitative expert opinion and additional quantitative metrics, such as faculty size, undergraduate and PhD student graduation rates, PhD placement, test-of-time awards, other awards, etc., but it is a start. Here are the data and scripts and the users' guide. We welcome corrections and feedback via github pull requests and issues. See motivation and methodology for more details.

Introduction to the metrics and data This web page computes institutional publication metrics for computer science configured by users. These metrics are principally focused on institutions, as opposed to faculty or authors, and for use in assessing both the past and predicting future research impact of publications. We cleaned publication, venue, and institution data from DBLP and Microsoft Academic for 2007 to 2016 appearing in 210 venues from 199 institutions. For each paper in this corpus, we divide credit equally among all authors and their institutions at time of publication. A publication never changes institutions and all authors (graduate students, post docs, faculty, undergraduates, etc.) accrue credit to their institution.

Users combine a measured (retrospective) and prediction (prospective) metric to compute an institutional measure. The measured metric uses citation counts and includes all citations at any time to papers published in the specified year range. The prediction metric counts papers in an independently specified year range and weighs them by venue. A venue weight of one values all venues equally. The geometric mean venue weight assigns each venue the geometric mean of citations to papers appearing in the venue from 2007-2016, thus more highly cited venues weigh more. (Venue weight is displayed next to selected venues.)

Users select year ranges for each metric and a venue weight. We suggest a disjoint range for the two metrics and that prediction only be used on recent publications that have not had sufficient time to accrue citations (e.g., 2014-2016). Users select all topics or individual topics to determine venues and may exclude/include individual venues to create custom venue lists. Since the metrics are not comparable, the combined metric uses geometric mean, where the user assigns a relative weight (alpha) to the measured and predictive metrics.

Please see the quick start guide for more help.
